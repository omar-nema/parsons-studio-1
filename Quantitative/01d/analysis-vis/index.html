How We Gaze: A Proof of Concept

Introduction

Imagine a piece of art that you love. How do you look at it? Do you study the work bit-by-bit? Or do admire it from a distance, and take in as a whole?

How would someone else look at the same work? Which elements of the piece is their attention drawn to?

This project attempts to visualize how individuals study a particular piece of art. This project serves a proof-of-concept for a broader series of work that examines how we look. Eye tracking data from five individuals was collected and visualized.

Experiment & Data Collection

For this project, I asked a group of participants (friends and peers) to examine a piece of art for 1 minute. I choose 'Local Calm' by Julie Mehretu as the piece to examine.

Ideally, I would have asked participants to view a variety of works. However, I wanted to narrow scope for this initial study, and choose to use a single piece in order to simplify the study. The selected Mehretu piece is complex and layered - one that would likely hold viewers' attention, and lead to a variety of different viewing patterns.

This piece was sourced from the Smithsonian American Art Museum, Renwick Art Gallery - link here.

Mehretu's 'Local Calm'

Participant eye movements were tracked using the webgazer.js library and sent to me via a downloaded JSON file. The data collection portion of the project is available here.

Each dataset contains a unique id for the participant, and an indication a set of x and y coordinates for each 0.1 seconds of data collection.

As part of this proof-of-concept, I asked participants to only send data if their accuracy rate was greater than 70%. However, I did not actually record the accuracy rate, so it is likely that some of the data is unreliable.

Numerical Analysis

Let’s look at some quick, cold stats to just understand the general nature of eye movement in this experiment. We’ll then delve into a more visual analysis in the next section.

On average, each viewers eyes traveled about the width of the image in a minute.

Though there was a lot of eye movement overall, much of this movement was concentrated in certain regions of the image. The contour map below highlights the regions with the most recorded points. You can see that the center-left is given the most attention.

The contour map below was created by interpolating between all recorded points in the dataset. Areas with dense small points get lines drawn around.
